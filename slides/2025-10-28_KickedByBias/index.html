Kicked by Bias

Ethnicity-Based Discrimination in German Soccer

Maurice Baudet von Gersdorff

October 28, 2025

Introduction

In soccer, “it doesn’t matter what nationalities, cultures, languages, or religious communities people come from.”1

(Maria Böhmer,2 as cited in Deutscher Olympischer Sportbund e. V. 2009; see also Bundesregierung der Bundesrepublik Deutschland 2011; Deutscher Fußball-Bund e. V. 2019; and Köttker et al. 2011)

  1. German original: Beim Fußball “ist es egal, aus welchen […] Nationalitäten, Kulturen, Sprachbereichen oder Religionsgemeinschaften die Menschen kommen.”

  2. Federal Government’s Commissioner for Migration, Refugees, and Integration, 2005–2013

Research Question

Puzzle

Why should ethnicity-based discrimination not matter in (amateur) soccer, a competitive environment institutionally framed as fair and meritocratic?

Research Question

Does (amateur) soccer, often celebrated as a space of social integration, reproduce structural forms of racial inequality?

Existing Literature

  • Magistro & Wack (2023): Players with darker skin tones receive about 20% more foul calls compared to lighter-skinned players in the Italian Serie A.

  • Gallo et al. (2013): White referees award more yellow cards against non-white players in the English Premier League.

  • Price & Wolfers (2010): In the National Basketball Association, players receive up to 4% fewer foul calls the more referees are of their own race.

Theory

  • Social Identity Theory: Ethnicity-based in-group/out-group categorization (Tajfel & Turner 2004).

  • Institutional pressures: Minority members may enforce stricter standards on their own group to prove legitimacy (Eiserloh et al. 2020).

  • Accountability: Scrutiny and high stakes incentives reduce bias (Parsons et al. 2011; Rickman & Witt 2008).

Argument

  • Out-group bias: Social Identity Theory predicts that referees may penalize players of other ethnicities.

  • Institutional pressures: Minority referees may overcompensate by sanctioning members of their own group more harshly.

  • Accountability: High scrutiny in professional leagues reduces referee bias; weaker oversight in amateur leagues allows discrimination.

Hypotheses

  • H1: White referees issue more disciplinary cards to teams perceived as having a higher share of non-white players than to teams perceived as having a lower share of non-white players.

  • H2a/b (competing): Non-white referees issue more disciplinary cards to teams perceived as having a higher share of white (non-white) players than to teams perceived as having a lower share of white (non-white) players.

  • H3: The effects observed in H1 and H2a/b are stronger in amateur soccer than in professional soccer.

Research Design

Quasi-Experimental Panel Design

  • Uses Fixed Effects Counterfactuals (Liu et al. 2024; see also Chiu et al. 2025) to exploit within-team variation in referee ethnicity across matches.

  • Each team moves in and out of treatment depending on the assigned referee.

  • No strict parallel-trends assumption; heterogeneous trends captured via interactive fixed effects.

  • Controls for team-, and match-level unobserved heterogeneity.

Data Source

  • A completely new dataset compiled by Klüver, Riaz, and Baudet von Gersdorff.

  • Contains data on about 1 million soccer games and 900,000 players from the 2022/23 to 2024/25 seasons.1

  • Includes information on the results of soccer matches and their exact course.

  • Uses AI to identify the perceived ethnicity of players, coaches, and referees based on their names and images.

  1. According to its own figures, the Deutscher Fußball-Bund e. V. (2025) organizes a total of around 1.4 million soccer matches per season.

Limitations

  • As data on disciplinary cards are only available at the team level, the analysis relies on team-level measures of ethnic composition.

  • Broad ethnicity identification neglects some interethnic conflict (e.g., Kurdish-Turkish, Russian-Ukrainian).1

  • Ethnicity approximation for referees is name-based only.

  • The mechanism is inferred theoretically only as referee’s attitudes or motivations are not directly observed.

  1. Credit and thanks to Evelyn for this point.

Contributions

Academic

  • First systematic study of ethnicity-based discrimination in German amateur and professional soccer.

  • Compares amateur versus professional settings in sports for the first time.

Societal

  • Raises awareness of ethnicity-based disparities in soccer.

  • Highlights bias in everyday sports, not just elite leagues.

Policy Implications

  • Diversity: Encourage recruitment of non-white referees.

  • Training: Implement bias-awareness programs.

  • Monitoring: Monitor disciplinary data to detect disparities

  • Transparency: Publish structured disciplinary data to identify disparities.
